The design of the landing page was initially created by Aleksa, and I continued working with the same theme. At first, the page featured an egg inside a CD, which I intended to use as an interactive "easter egg." The idea was that when clicked, it would reveal three options, and selecting the correct one would provide a discount for merchandise or concert tickets. However, after receiving feedback that the design didn’t need to include a literal easter egg and should instead connect more closely with the band, I began brainstorming alternative concepts.

In the second version of the landing page, I included a "My Tickets" button. However, since the website was designed for a single event, having an account system was unnecessary. By the third version, I integrated a red CD with a portal leading to ticket purchases. After conducting user testing, my teacher pointed out that the spinning CDs were distracting and that users would prefer to see more information—such as the event location—before proceeding to buy tickets.

This feedback led to the final version of the landing page, which we later coded. The spinning CDs remained but were designed to focus user attention on them. A "Learn More" button was added, directing users to detailed event information. Initially, I planned to include a clickable content table for navigation but decided it would be too complex for users. Instead, I opted for a simpler design: an image containing all relevant information. The countdown feature was also incorporated alongside a prominent "Get Your Tickets" button.

For social media integration, I initially added a static image of their Instagram page but later pivoted to a more creative solution. While browsing Canva, I came across a gallery room concept and decided to recreate it in Blender for a visually engaging experience. Additionally, I developed supporting pages such as "About Us," "Location," and "FAQ."

During user testing with three participants, feedback on the spinning CDs from the landing page was particularly insightful. When Aleksa first presented the idea, I thought it would be engaging if hovering over them caused them to spin and play music. However, due to time constraints, only the spinning functionality was implemented.

Overall, this iterative process allowed me to refine the website's design and functionality based on user feedback and testing while ensuring it aligned with both user needs and the band's identity. Click here for Canva prototype

In the previous project, I felt that I did not conduct as many interviews as I would have liked, so for this project, I made it a priority to interview four users. During these interviews, I asked questions that would provide insights for the website, and afterward, I highlighted everything that would be useful for the design. The interviews were particularly helpful in determining the language for the website. Initially, I considered creating both English and Dutch versions, but most interviewees indicated that many people today understand English well enough that a separate Dutch version might not be necessary. They also mentioned that there are extensions available that can translate content automatically. Additionally, the participants expressed that having a countdown timer and clear information about the event would be beneficial features. Interestingly, no one mentioned the idea of an easter egg as a necessary element; while they recognized it as a common feature on event websites, they felt it was not essential. The interviews were important in creating the website because they provided direct feedback from potential users. This feedback allowed me to focus on what features and information were truly important to them, ensuring that the final design would meet their needs and expectations effectively. By understanding their preferences and concerns, I could create a more user-friendly experience that resonates with the target audience.Click here for interview pdf

First, I looked online to understand what a new wave punk band’s aesthetic is like. I explored their website and Instagram page to see the colors they typically use and the themes of their lyrics. I wanted to get a sense of what they sing about most often. After that, I went to TikTok to observe how fans of this type of music express themselves—how they dress, how their rooms are decorated, and what they like. I noticed that their style is heavily influenced by vintage items, with lots of bright colors and unique, unusual choices. Their rooms are filled with posters, concert tickets, lights, pictures, and drawings—often featuring a colorful or old-paper look. For my mood board, I included an egg and chicken, symbolizing the origins of the band’s songwriting process. I also added an image of a girl with green hair, as many fans I saw have brightly colored hair. Overall, I found that the aesthetic is chaotic and vibrant, rather than clean and simple. When I showed my teacher my posters, he mentioned that he prefers a more polished, one-color style and showed me examples of his work, which are very professional and well put together. However, I believe that professionalism doesn’t always mean clean and orderly. For a new wave punk band, the goal is to capture the raw, expressive energy of their style. If I were working with this band, I would want to create posters that reflect my own interpretation of their aesthetic, embracing the chaotic and bold vibe that defines their look and sound.

New wave is a music genre that encompasses pop-oriented styles from the 1970s through the 1980s. It is considered a lighter and more melodic "broadening of punk culture "New wave music is a broad subgenre of rock 'n' roll that combines elements of punk rock, mainstream pop music, art rock, synth pop, funk, and reggae.

Because the band mentioned that they want to attract a younger generation, I did some research on what new generation actually likes and what is trendy nowadays. I started by looking at the overall most popular and trendy things. One example I came across was the “Rhode” photo booth that Hailey Bieber created in London to promote her new lip gloss. With each photo taken in the booth, people received a lip gloss, and thousands of people queued up from morning till night to take a picture. The "Life4Cut" photo booth, which is really popular among K-pop fans, also caught my attention. People can take photos with the theme of their favorite K-pop bands, and it draws huge crowds.

Another trend that’s really popular is Spotify. Almost everyone uses it now, and most people won’t listen to music unless it’s available on Spotify because they rarely want to install another app just to listen to a single song. The band already has their music on Spotify, but to make it even more popular, many people have the code to scan and go directly to the music.

Looking at the Instagram pages of some of the most popular singers, like Travis Scott and Central Cee, who are really popular among the younger generation, I noticed a few trends. Their Instagram feeds often feature two main colors that pop when you see the photo, and the feed usually has 9 photos in total. They also use handwritten fonts and old paper-like posts, and often share behind-the-scenes moments.

For people into new wave, punk, and similar music genres, I researched TikTok and found that this group also likes vintage products. Their color palette tends to lean towards brownish tones or chaotic mixes of neon colors, with red and yellow being especially popular. Interestingly, every girl I interviewed, even those into this style of music, mentioned they like pink, but there was a split—some prefer hot pink, while others like a softer shade of pink.
