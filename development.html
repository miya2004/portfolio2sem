For analysis, I examined websites like Ticketmaster, Imagine Dragons' website, and StubHub. In the photo, I circled everything that I would like to include in my website. These websites are for events and concerts, and there are several pieces of information that each of them includes. Ticketmaster is the most popular, and I got the design for the location and some ideas from there. They also had a countdown, which I made sure to include as well. Since our client's website is for one specific event, I didn't include categories or other tabs but rather included everything in one scrollable page for easier navigation.

In my previous project, I felt I didn't conduct as many interviews as I wanted. For this project, I interviewed four people, asking questions that would help me with the website design. I highlighted everything that I planned to use in the website from these interviews. The interviews also helped me decide on the language for the website. I was considering both English and Dutch versions, but most interviewees said that nowadays, many people know English, so a Dutch version isn't really necessary. They also mentioned that there are extensions that can translate automatically. The interviewees emphasized that a counter and clear information for the event are important to have. The design of the landing page was initially created by Aleksa, and I continued working with the same theme. At first, the page featured an egg inside a CD, which I intended to use as an interactive "easter egg." The idea was that when clicked, it would reveal three options, and selecting the correct one would provide a discount for merchandise or concert tickets. However, after receiving feedback that the design didn’t need to include a literal easter egg and should instead connect more closely with the band, I began brainstorming alternative concepts.

In the second version of the landing page, I included a "My Tickets" button. However, since the website was designed for a single event, having an account system was unnecessary. By the third version, I integrated a red CD with a portal leading to ticket purchases. After conducting user testing, my teacher pointed out that the spinning CDs were distracting and that users would prefer to see more information—such as the event location—before proceeding to buy tickets.

This feedback led to the final version of the landing page, which we later coded. The spinning CDs remained but were designed to focus user attention on them. A "Learn More" button was added, directing users to detailed event information. Initially, I planned to include a clickable content table for navigation but decided it would be too complex for users. Instead, I opted for a simpler design: an image containing all relevant information. The countdown feature was also incorporated alongside a prominent "Get Your Tickets" button.

For social media integration, I initially added a static image of their Instagram page but later pivoted to a more creative solution. While browsing Canva, I came across a gallery room concept and decided to recreate it in Blender for a visually engaging experience. Additionally, I developed supporting pages such as "About Us," "Location," and "FAQ."

During user testing with three participants, feedback on the spinning CDs from the landing page was particularly insightful. When Aleksa first presented the idea, I thought it would be engaging if hovering over them caused them to spin and play music. However, due to time constraints, only the spinning functionality was implemented.

Overall, this iterative process allowed me to refine the website's design and functionality based on user feedback and testing while ensuring it aligned with both user needs and the band's identity. Click here to see the prototype in Canva.
